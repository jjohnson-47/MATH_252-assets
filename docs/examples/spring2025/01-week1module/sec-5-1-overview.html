Topic: Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integration

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of antiderivatives and indefinite integration, laying the groundwork for our study of integral calculus.

In this section, we introduce the concept of an antiderivative and the idea of indefinite integration. We begin by revisiting differential equations: an equation that involves a function \( f \), the variable \( x \), and the derivatives of \( f \).

For example, a simple differential equation is \( \frac{dF}{dx} = 1 \). Solving this means finding all functions \( F(x) \) such that \( F'(x) = 1 \). You might find one solution: \( F(x) = x \). But you can also see that \( F(x) = x + 7 \) works, and so does \( F(x) = x - 5 \), etc. We quickly realize there is not just the antiderivative, but infinitely many, differing only by a constant.

Definition 5.1.1: Antiderivatives & Indefinite Integrals

Let a function \( f(x) \) be given. An antiderivative of \( f \) is a function \( F(x) \) such that \( F'(x) = f(x) \).

The set of all antiderivatives of \( f \) is called the indefinite integral of \( f \), denoted:

\[ \int f(x)\,dx. \]

We note there are infinitely many antiderivatives, so we refer to an antiderivative rather than the antiderivative of \( f \). Often, we use uppercase letters like \( F \) to denote any antiderivative of \( f \).

When \( f \) is continuous, knowing one antiderivative of \( f \) allows us to find infinitely many more simply by adding a constant \( C \). In fact, this gives us all antiderivatives of \( f \).

Theorem 5.1.2: Antiderivative Forms

Let \( G \) and \( H \) be antiderivatives of a continuous function \( f \) on an interval \( I \). Then there exists a constant \( C \) such that, on \( I \),

\[ G(x) = H(x) + C. \]

Equivalently, if \( F \) is any antiderivative of \( f \) on \( I \), then the set of all antiderivatives is given by:

\[ F(x) + C, \quad \text{for any real constant } C. \]

We also introduce the standard notation of the indefinite integral:

\[ \int f(x)\, dx. \]

The symbol \(\int\) (an elongated ‚ÄúS‚Äù for ‚Äúsum‚Äù) and the differential \(dx\) combine to mean ‚Äúfind all antiderivatives of \( f(x) \).‚Äù You can think of it as ‚Äúundoing‚Äù differentiation with respect to the variable \( x \).

Example 5.1.4: Evaluating an Indefinite Integral

Evaluate \( \int 1 \, dx. \)

Solution: We look for all functions whose derivative is 1. A family of solutions is \( x + C \), where \( C \) is any real constant. So: \[ \int 1 \, dx = x + C. \]

One might ask, ‚ÄúWhere did the \( dx \) go?‚Äù From a deeper perspective, we‚Äôre solving the differential equation \( dF = dx \), which asks ‚Äúwhat function \( F \) has the differential form \( dF = dx \)?‚Äù

Example 5.1.5: Evaluating an Indefinite Integral

Evaluate \( \int \bigl( x^2 + 5x - 7 \bigr)\, dx.\)

Solution:

We can use the Sum/Difference Rule and the Power Rule of integration: \[ \int x^2\,dx = \frac{x^3}{3},\quad \int 5x\,dx = \frac{5x^2}{2},\quad \int (-7)\,dx = -7x. \] Putting it all together:

\[ \int ( x^2 + 5x - 7 ) \, dx = \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{5x^2}{2} - 7x + C. \]

Always verify by differentiating your result. Indeed, \(\frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{5x^2}{2} - 7x\bigr) = x^2 + 5x -7\).

We restate part of our earlier rules from differentiation, now highlighting the relationship with antiderivatives:

Theorem 5.1.6: Common Derivatives & Antiderivatives

Here are some standard pairs of derivatives and indefinite integrals:

  • Constant Multiple Rule: \(\displaystyle \int k \cdot f(x)\,dx = k \int f(x)\,dx.\)
  • Sum/Difference Rule: \(\displaystyle \int \bigl(f(x) \pm g(x)\bigr)\,dx = \int f(x)\,dx \pm \int g(x)\,dx.\)
  • Power Rule: \(\displaystyle \int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C,\quad n \neq -1.\)
  • Log Rule: \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln\lvert x\rvert + C.\)

In short, antidifferentiation is the inverse of differentiation. Remember the saying: ‚ÄúInverse operations do the opposite things in the opposite order.‚Äù

We can apply these ideas to ‚Äúinitial value problems,‚Äù where we solve a differential equation with a given initial condition.

Example 5.1.7: Initial Value Problem

Suppose the acceleration of a falling object is \(-32 \text{ ft/s}^2\). At time \( t = 0 \), let the velocity be \(-10 \text{ ft/s}\). Find the velocity \(v(t)\).

Solution: We have \( \frac{dv}{dt} = -32 \). Integrate: \[ v(t) = \int -32 \, dt = -32t + C. \] Use the condition \( v(0) = -10 \): \[ v(0) = -32\cdot 0 + C = -10 \quad\Rightarrow\quad C = -10. \] Thus, \[ v(t) = -32t - 10. \]

Example 5.1.8: Another Initial Value Problem

Find \( F(x) \), given \( \frac{dF}{dx} = 3x^2 - 4 \) and \( F(1) = 5 \).

Solution: \[ F(x) = \int \bigl(3x^2 - 4\bigr)\,dx = 3 \cdot \frac{x^3}{3} - 4x + C = x^3 - 4x + C. \] Use \( F(1) = 5 \): \[ F(1) = 1^3 - 4\cdot 1 + C = 5 \quad\Rightarrow\quad C = 8. \] So, \[ F(x) = x^3 - 4x + 8. \]
