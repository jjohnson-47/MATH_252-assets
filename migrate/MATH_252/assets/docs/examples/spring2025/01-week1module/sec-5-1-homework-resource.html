Calculating Constant Acceleration and Distance Graph

Problem

A car is accelerating from 30 mi/h to 50 mi/h in 5 seconds. To solve this problem, you will need to:

  1. Calculate the constant acceleration required for the car to increase its speed from \(30\) mi/h to \(50\) mi/h in \(5\) seconds.
  2. Sketch a graph showing the distance traveled by the car, \( s(t) \), during this \(5\)-second period.

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the Constant Acceleration

First, we need to calculate the constant acceleration \( a \) using the kinematic equation for velocity with constant acceleration:

\[ v_f = v_i + a t \]

Where:

  • \( v_i \) is the initial velocity.
  • \( v_f \) is the final velocity.
  • \( t \) is the time elapsed.
  • \( a \) is the acceleration.

Note: We need to convert velocities from miles per hour to feet per second for consistency in units.

Converting velocities:

\[ v_i = 30 \text{ mi/h} \times \frac{5280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}} \times \frac{1 \text{ h}}{3600 \text{ s}} = 44 \text{ ft/s} \] \[ v_f = 50 \text{ mi/h} \times \frac{5280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}} \times \frac{1 \text{ h}}{3600 \text{ s}} \approx 73.3 \text{ ft/s} \]

Now, we can solve for \( a \):

\[ a = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t} \] \[ a = \frac{73.3 \text{ ft/s} - 44 \text{ ft/s}}{5 \text{ s}} = \frac{29.3 \text{ ft/s}}{5 \text{ s}} \approx 5.86 \text{ ft/s}^2 \]

Answer: The constant acceleration required is approximately \( 5.86 \text{ ft/s}^2 \).

Step 2: Sketch the Distance Graph

Next, we will find the equation for the distance traveled \( s(t) \) over time \( t \) using the kinematic equation for position:

\[ s(t) = v_i t + \tfrac{1}{2} a t^2 \]

Substitute the known values:

\[ s(t) = 44 t + \tfrac{1}{2} (5.86) t^2 \] \[ s(t) = 44 t + 2.93 t^2 \]

This quadratic equation represents the distance traveled over time during the acceleration period.

Understanding the Concepts:

  • Acceleration is the rate at which velocity changes with time. A constant acceleration means the velocity changes uniformly over time.
  • The kinematic equations are essential for solving problems involving constant acceleration in one dimension.
  • Converting units is crucial to ensure consistency, especially when using standard equations that require specific units.
  • The distance function \( s(t) \) is quadratic because acceleration contributes a \( t^2 \) term, indicating that distance increases quadratically over time when acceleration is constant.

By calculating the constant acceleration and graphing the distance function, we've analyzed the car's motion during its acceleration from \(30\) mi/h to \(50\) mi/h over \(5\) seconds. This exercise demonstrates the application of kinematic equations in solving real-world problems.
