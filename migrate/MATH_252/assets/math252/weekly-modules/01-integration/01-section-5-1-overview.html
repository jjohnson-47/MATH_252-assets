Introduction to Antiderivatives

In this section, we're looking at the process of finding functions when we know their derivatives. This is the reverse of what we've been doing in differential calculus, and it's the foundation of integral calculus.

Consider a simple differential equation: \( \frac{dF}{dx} = 1 \). What function \( F(x) \) has a derivative of 1? You might think of \( F(x) = x \). But what about \( F(x) = x + 7 \)? Or \( F(x) = x - 5 \)? All of these functions have a derivative of 1. We quickly realize that there isn't just one antiderivative, but infinitely many, all differing by a constant.

Definition: Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integrals

Let a function \( f(x) \) be given. An antiderivative of \( f \) is a function \( F(x) \) such that \( F'(x) = f(x) \).

The set of all antiderivatives of \( f \) is called the indefinite integral of \( f \), denoted:

\[ \int f(x)\,dx \]

See full definition in the textbook

We refer to an antiderivative rather than the antiderivative of \( f \) because there are infinitely many. When \( f \) is continuous, all antiderivatives differ by a constant.

Theorem 5.1.2: Antiderivative Forms

Let \( G \) and \( H \) be antiderivatives of a continuous function \( f \) on an interval \( I \). Then there exists a constant \( C \) such that, on \( I \):

\[ G(x) = H(x) + C \]

If \( F \) is any antiderivative of \( f \) on \( I \), then the set of all antiderivatives is given by \( F(x) + C \), for any real constant \( C \).

See full theorem in the textbook

Notation and Interpretation

The standard notation for the indefinite integral is:

\[ \int f(x)\, dx \]

The symbol \(\int\) (an elongated "S" for "sum") and the differential \(dx\) combine to mean "find all antiderivatives of \( f(x) \)." You can think of it as "undoing" differentiation with respect to \( x \).

Example: Basic Indefinite Integral

Evaluate \( \int 1 \, dx \)

Solution:

We're looking for all functions whose derivative is 1. A family of such functions is \( x + C \), where \( C \) is any real constant. So:

\[ \int 1 \, dx = x + C \]

One might ask, "Where did the \( dx \) go?" From a deeper perspective, we're solving the differential equation \( dF = dx \), which asks "what function \( F \) has the differential form \( dF = dx \)?"

Example: Polynomial Integration

Evaluate \( \int (x^2 + 5x - 7) \, dx \)

Solution:

We can split this up using the Sum/Difference Rule:

\begin{align} \int x^2\,dx &= \frac{x^3}{3} + C_1\\ \int 5x\,dx &= \frac{5x^2}{2} + C_2\\ \int (-7)\,dx &= -7x + C_3 \end{align}

Combining these results and using a single constant \( C = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 \):

\[ \int (x^2 + 5x - 7) \, dx = \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{5x^2}{2} - 7x + C \]

Always verify by differentiating your result: \(\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{5x^2}{2} - 7x + C\right) = x^2 + 5x - 7\).

Integration Rules

Theorem 5.1.6: Common Integration Rules

Here are fundamental rules for indefinite integrals:

  1. Constant Multiple Rule: \(\displaystyle \int k \cdot f(x)\,dx = k \int f(x)\,dx\)

Initial Value Problems

We can apply antiderivatives to "initial value problems," where we solve differential equations with given initial conditions.

Example 5.1.8: Solving an Initial Value Problem

Find \( F(x) \), given that \( \frac{dF}{dx} = 3x^2 - 4 \) and \( F(1) = 5 \).

Solution:

First, we find the general form of \(F(x)\) by integration:

\begin{align} F(x) &= \int (3x^2 - 4)\,dx\\ &= 3 \int x^2\,dx - 4\int dx\\ &= 3 \cdot \frac{x^3}{3} - 4x + C\\ &= x^3 - 4x + C \end{align}

Now we use the initial condition \( F(1) = 5 \) to find the specific value of \(C\):

\begin{align} F(1) &= 1^3 - 4(1) + C = 5\\ 1 - 4 + C &= 5\\ C &= 8 \end{align}

Therefore, the particular solution is:

\[ F(x) = x^3 - 4x + 8 \]

See full example in the textbook

Key Takeaways

  • Antiderivatives reverse the process of differentiation
  • For a continuous function, all antiderivatives differ by a constant
  • The indefinite integral \(\int f(x)\,dx\) represents the family of all antiderivatives of \(f(x)\)
  • Always include the arbitrary constant \(C\) when writing indefinite integrals
  • Use initial conditions to find specific values for the constant \(C\)
  • Verify your answers by differentiating the result
