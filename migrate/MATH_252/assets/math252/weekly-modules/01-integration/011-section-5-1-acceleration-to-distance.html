Application of Calculus: Motion Analysis

This example demonstrates how integration concepts connect to real-world problems involving motion. A velocity function can be integrated to find a position function, which gives us distance traveled over time.

When acceleration is constant, velocity changes linearly with time, and distance (position) follows a quadratic function. This relationship illustrates a fundamental connection between derivatives and anti-derivatives in calculus.

Problem

A car is accelerating from 30 mi/h to 50 mi/h in 5 seconds.

  1. Calculate the constant acceleration required for the car to achieve this change in velocity.
  2. Find the function that represents the distance traveled by the car during this 5-second period.
  3. Determine how far the car travels during these 5 seconds of acceleration.
Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration
\begin{align} v(t) &= v_0 + at \\ s(t) &= s_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \end{align}

Where:

  • \(v(t)\) = velocity at time \(t\)
  • \(v_0\) = initial velocity
  • \(a\) = constant acceleration
  • \(s(t)\) = position at time \(t\)
  • \(s_0\) = initial position (we'll use \(s_0 = 0\))

Step 1: Convert Units to a Consistent System

Since we're working with acceleration and distance, we need to convert the speeds from miles per hour to a more appropriate unit. In physics problems, it's common to use feet per second (ft/s) in the U.S. customary system.

\begin{align} v_0 &= 30 \text{ mi/h} \times \frac{5280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}} \times \frac{1 \text{ h}}{3600 \text{ s}} \\ &= 30 \times 5280 \times \frac{1}{3600} \text{ ft/s} \\ &= 30 \times \frac{5280}{3600} \text{ ft/s} \\ &= 30 \times 1.47 \text{ ft/s} \\ &= 44 \text{ ft/s} \end{align}
\begin{align} v_f &= 50 \text{ mi/h} \times \frac{5280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}} \times \frac{1 \text{ h}}{3600 \text{ s}} \\ &= 50 \times \frac{5280}{3600} \text{ ft/s} \\ &= 50 \times 1.47 \text{ ft/s} \\ &= 73.3 \text{ ft/s} \end{align}

So we need to find the acceleration that changes the car's velocity from 44 ft/s to 73.3 ft/s over 5 seconds.

Step 2: Calculate the Constant Acceleration

Using the kinematic equation for velocity with constant acceleration:

\[ v(t) = v_0 + at \]

We can rearrange this to solve for acceleration:

\begin{align} a &= \frac{v(t) - v_0}{t} \\ &= \frac{v_f - v_0}{t_f - t_0} \\ &= \frac{73.3 \text{ ft/s} - 44 \text{ ft/s}}{5 \text{ s} - 0 \text{ s}} \\ &= \frac{29.3 \text{ ft/s}}{5 \text{ s}} \\ &= 5.86 \text{ ft/s}^2 \end{align}

The constant acceleration required is 5.86 ft/s² (approximately 5.9 ft/s²).

This means the car's velocity increases by 5.86 feet per second every second during this time interval.

Step 3: Find the Distance Function

Now that we know the acceleration, we can find the position function \(s(t)\) using the kinematic equation:

\[ s(t) = s_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

If we set the initial position \(s_0 = 0\) (measuring distance from the starting point), we get:

\begin{align} s(t) &= v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \\ &= 44t + \frac{1}{2}(5.86)t^2 \\ &= 44t + 2.93t^2 \end{align}

This is the equation that describes the distance the car travels as a function of time during the 5-second acceleration period.

From a calculus perspective, \(s(t)\) is the antiderivative of the velocity function \(v(t) = 44 + 5.86t\), illustrating the fundamental relationship between position and velocity.

Step 4: Calculate the Total Distance Traveled

To find the total distance traveled during the 5-second period, we evaluate \(s(t)\) at \(t = 5\):

\begin{align} s(5) &= 44(5) + 2.93(5)^2 \\ &= 220 + 2.93(25) \\ &= 220 + 73.25 \\ &= 293.25 \text{ ft} \end{align}

The car travels approximately 293 feet during the 5-second acceleration period.

That's nearly the length of a football field in just 5 seconds!

Calculus Connections

This problem demonstrates several important calculus concepts:

  1. Integration Relationship: The position function \(s(t)\) is the antiderivative (indefinite integral) of the velocity function \(v(t)\).
    \[s(t) = \int v(t)\,dt = \int (v_0 + at)\,dt = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 + C\]
    Setting the constant of integration \(C = 0\) gives us our position function.
  2. Rate of Change: The acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, and velocity is the rate of change of position:
    \[a = \frac{dv}{dt}, \quad v = \frac{ds}{dt}\]
  3. Quadratic Function: When acceleration is constant, position follows a quadratic function, which we see in the graph.

Understanding these relationships is fundamental to analyzing motion problems using calculus.
