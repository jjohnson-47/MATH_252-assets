The Big Picture: Reversing the Chain Rule

Integration by substitution is our first major technique for handling complex integrals. The key insight: substitution reverses the Chain Rule, allowing us to integrate composite functions that would otherwise be impossible to handle.

Just as the Chain Rule helps us differentiate compositions like $f(g(x))$, substitution helps us integrate expressions that arise from differentiating composite functions.

The Foundation: Chain Rule Connection

How Substitution Reverses the Chain Rule

The Chain Rule tells us that:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\Big[F\big(g(x)\big)\Big] = F'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Reading this backwards: If we can recognize an integrand of the form $F'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$, then we know its antiderivative is $F(g(x)) + C$.

The substitution process: When we set $u = g(x)$, we have $du = g'(x)\,dx$, and our integral becomes:

$$\int F'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\,dx = \int F'(u)\,du = F(u) + C = F(g(x)) + C$$

When to Use Substitution: Pattern Recognition

Look for these key elements in the integrand:

  • Composite function: A function nested inside another function
  • Derivative present: The derivative (or a constant multiple) of the inner function appears elsewhere in the integrand
  • Simplification potential: The integral becomes easier after substitution

Common patterns: $\int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\,dx$, where recognizing $g(x)$ and $g'(x)$ is the key to success.

Understanding Differentials: The Foundation of Substitution

Why can we write $du = f'(x) \, dx$? This notation might seem mysterious, but it has a solid mathematical foundation.

When we define $u = f(x)$, the differential $du$ represents an infinitesimal change in $u$ corresponding to an infinitesimal change $dx$ in $x$.

The mathematical justification:

  • From the definition of the derivative: $\frac{du}{dx} = f'(x)$
  • Multiplying both sides by $dx$: $\frac{du}{dx} \cdot dx = f'(x) \cdot dx$
  • This gives us: $du = f'(x) \, dx$

Interpretation: If $x$ changes by a small amount $dx$, then $u$ changes by approximately $f'(x) \, dx$. In the context of integration, this differential relationship allows us to transform $dx$ into $du$, making substitution possible.

Fundamental Examples

Example 1: Basic Substitution with Color-Coding (Based on Example 6.1.2)

Problem: Evaluate $\int x \cdot \cos(x^2) \, dx$.

Step 1: Identify the pattern and choose substitution.

We see $\cos(x^2)$ (a composite function) and $x$ (related to the derivative of $x^2$).

Let's choose $\textcolor{blue}{u = x^2}$ as our substitution.

Step 2: Find the differential relationship.

If $\textcolor{blue}{u = x^2}$, then $\frac{du}{dx} = 2x$, so $du = 2x \, dx$.

We need $x \, dx$, so we solve: $\textcolor{red}{\frac{1}{2} du = x \, dx}$.

Step 3: Replace everything in terms of $u$ using color-coding.

$$\int \textcolor{red}{x} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{\cos(x^2)} \, \textcolor{red}{dx} = \textcolor{red}{\frac{1}{2}} \int \textcolor{blue}{\cos(u)} \, \textcolor{red}{du}$$

Step 4: Integrate with respect to $u$.

$$\frac{1}{2}\int \cos(u)\,du = \frac{1}{2}\sin(u) + C$$

Step 5: Convert back to the original variable.

$$\frac{1}{2}\sin(u) + C = \frac{1}{2}\sin(x^2) + C$$

Example 2: Definite Integration with Substitution (Based on Example 6.1.7)

Problem: Evaluate $\int_0^2 x\sqrt{x^2 + 1}\,dx$.

Method 1: Change the limits of integration

Let $u = x^2 + 1$, so $du = 2x\,dx$ and $\frac{1}{2}du = x\,dx$.

Change limits: When $x = 0$: $u = 0^2 + 1 = 1$. When $x = 2$: $u = 2^2 + 1 = 5$.

Transform the integral:

$$\int_0^2 x\sqrt{x^2 + 1}\,dx = \int_1^5 \sqrt{u} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\,du = \frac{1}{2}\int_1^5 u^{1/2}\,du$$

Evaluate:

$$\frac{1}{2}\int_1^5 u^{1/2}\,du = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2}\Big|_1^5 = \frac{1}{3}\left[u^{3/2}\right]_1^5 = \frac{1}{3}(5^{3/2} - 1^{3/2}) = \frac{1}{3}(5\sqrt{5} - 1)$$

Example 3: When Substitution Leads to More Complexity

Problem: Evaluate $\int t^3 e^{-t^2} \, dt$.

Step 1: Identify a potential substitution.

We see $e^{-t^2}$ and notice that the derivative of $-t^2$ is $-2t$. This suggests trying $\textcolor{blue}{u = -t^2}$.

Step 2: Set up the substitution.

If $\textcolor{blue}{u = -t^2}$, then $\frac{du}{dt} = -2t$, so $du = -2t \, dt$.

Solving for $t \, dt$: $\textcolor{red}{t \, dt = -\frac{1}{2} du}$.

Step 3: Attempt the substitution.

We have $t^3 = t^2 \cdot t$, and since $u = -t^2$, we get $t^2 = -u$.

$$\int t^3 e^{-t^2} \, dt = \int t^2 \cdot t \cdot e^{-t^2} \, dt = \int (-u) \cdot \textcolor{blue}{e^u} \cdot \textcolor{red}{\left(-\frac{1}{2} du\right)}$$
$$= \frac{1}{2} \int u e^u \, du$$

Step 4: Recognize the new challenge.

Now we have $\int u e^u \, du$, which is a product of $u$ and $e^u$. This cannot be integrated using basic antiderivative rules!

This type of integral requires integration by parts, which we'll learn in Section 6.2.

Key insight: Substitution sometimes transforms one difficult integral into a different type of difficult integral. Building a toolbox of integration techniques allows us to tackle increasingly complex problems by combining methods.

Mastering Substitution:

  • Pattern recognition is crucial: Look for composite functions paired with derivatives of inner functions
  • Practice the process: Substitute, integrate, convert back
  • Check your work: Differentiate your answer to verify it matches the original integrand
  • For definite integrals: Either change limits or convert back to original variable
  • Don't force it: If substitution doesn't clearly simplify the integral, try a different approach
