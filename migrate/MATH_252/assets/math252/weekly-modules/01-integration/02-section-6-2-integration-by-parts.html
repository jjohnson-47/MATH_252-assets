Deriving Integration by Parts: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Start with the Product Rule for derivatives.

If we have two functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$, the Product Rule tells us:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\Big[f(x)g(x)\Big] = f(x) \cdot g'(x) + f'(x) \cdot g(x)$$

Step 2: Integrate both sides with respect to the independent variable $x$.

This gives us:

$$f(x)g(x) = \int f(x) \cdot g'(x) \, dx + \int f'(x) \cdot g(x) \, dx$$

Step 3: Solve for one of the integrals.

Rearranging to isolate the first integral:

$$\int f(x) \cdot g'(x) \, dx = f(x)g(x) - \int f'(x) \cdot g(x) \, dx$$

Step 4: Introduce compact notation.

Let $\textcolor{blue}{u = f(x)}$ and $\textcolor{red}{v = g(x)}$. Then:

  • $\textcolor{blue}{du = f'(x) \, dx}$ (differential of $u$)
  • $\textcolor{red}{dv = g'(x) \, dx}$ (differential of $v$)

Integration by Parts Formula:

$$\boxed{\int \textcolor{blue}{u} \, \textcolor{red}{dv} = \textcolor{blue}{u}\textcolor{red}{v} - \int \textcolor{red}{v} \, \textcolor{blue}{du}}$$

Definite Integration by Parts

For definite integrals, integration by parts becomes:

$$\int_a^b \textcolor{blue}{u} \, \textcolor{red}{dv} = \Big[\textcolor{blue}{u}\textcolor{red}{v}\Big]_a^b - \int_a^b \textcolor{red}{v} \, \textcolor{blue}{du}$$

Key advantage: We can evaluate the boundary term $[uv]_a^b$ directly without finding the indefinite integral first.

The LIATE Strategy

When using integration by parts, choose $\textcolor{blue}{u}$ based on the priority order LIATE:

  • Logarithmic functions ($\ln x$, $\log x$)
  • Inverse trigonometric functions ($\arcsin x$, $\arctan x$)
  • Algebraic functions ($x^n$, polynomials)
  • Trigonometric functions ($\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\tan x$)
  • Exponential functions ($e^x$, $a^x$)

Strategy: Choose $\textcolor{blue}{u}$ to be the function that appears earlier in this list, and $\textcolor{red}{dv}$ to be the remaining part of the integrand (including $dx$).

Fundamental Examples

Example 1: Basic Integration by Parts with Color-Coding

Problem: Evaluate $\int x e^x \, dx$.

Step 1: Choose $u$ and $dv$ using LIATE.

We have an algebraic function ($x$) and an exponential function ($e^x$). By LIATE, choose:

$\textcolor{blue}{u = x}$ (algebraic comes before exponential)

$\textcolor{red}{dv = e^x \, dx}$

Step 2: Find $du$ and $v$ by differentiation and integration.

$\textcolor{blue}{du = \frac{d}{dx}[x] \, dx = 1 \, dx = dx}$

$\textcolor{red}{v = \int e^x \, dx = e^x}$

Step 3: Apply the integration by parts formula with color-coding.

$$\int \textcolor{blue}{x} \cdot \textcolor{red}{e^x \, dx} = \textcolor{blue}{x} \cdot \textcolor{red}{e^x} - \int \textcolor{red}{e^x} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{dx}$$
$$= x e^x - \int e^x \, dx$$
$$= x e^x - e^x + C$$
$$= e^x(x - 1) + C$$

Check: $\frac{d}{dx}[e^x(x-1)] = e^x(x-1) + e^x \cdot 1 = e^x(x-1+1) = xe^x$ ✓

Example 2: Definite Integration by Parts

Problem: Evaluate $\int_0^1 x e^{2x} \, dx$.

Step 1: Choose $u$ and $dv$ using LIATE.

$\textcolor{blue}{u = x}$ (algebraic)

$\textcolor{red}{dv = e^{2x} \, dx}$ (exponential)

Step 2: Find $du$ and $v$.

$\textcolor{blue}{du = dx}$

$\textcolor{red}{v = \int e^{2x} \, dx = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x}}$

Step 3: Apply the definite integration by parts formula.

$$\int_0^1 \textcolor{blue}{x} \cdot \textcolor{red}{e^{2x} \, dx} = \Big[\textcolor{blue}{x} \cdot \textcolor{red}{\frac{1}{2}e^{2x}}\Big]_0^1 - \int_0^1 \textcolor{red}{\frac{1}{2}e^{2x}} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{dx}$$

Step 4: Evaluate the boundary term and remaining integral.

$$= \left[\frac{x e^{2x}}{2}\right]_0^1 - \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 e^{2x} \, dx$$
$$= \left(\frac{1 \cdot e^2}{2} - \frac{0 \cdot e^0}{2}\right) - \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left[\frac{e^{2x}}{2}\right]_0^1$$
$$= \frac{e^2}{2} - \frac{1}{4}(e^2 - e^0) = \frac{e^2}{2} - \frac{1}{4}(e^2 - 1)$$
$$= \frac{e^2}{2} - \frac{e^2}{4} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{e^2}{4} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{e^2 + 1}{4}$$

Example 3: When LIATE Doesn't Make the Choice Obvious

Problem: Evaluate $\int \ln x \, dx$.

Step 1: Recognize the challenge.

This integral contains only one function: $\ln x$. How do we apply integration by parts?

Key insight: Write $\int \ln x \, dx = \int \ln x \cdot 1 \, dx$

Step 2: Choose $u$ and $dv$ strategically.

We have logarithmic ($\ln x$) and algebraic ($1$). By LIATE:

$\textcolor{blue}{u = \ln x}$ (logarithmic comes first)

$\textcolor{red}{dv = 1 \, dx = dx}$ (the "hidden" algebraic function)

Step 3: Find $du$ and $v$.

$\textcolor{blue}{du = \frac{1}{x} \, dx}$

$\textcolor{red}{v = \int dx = x}$

Step 4: Apply integration by parts.

$$\int \textcolor{blue}{\ln x} \cdot \textcolor{red}{dx} = \textcolor{blue}{\ln x} \cdot \textcolor{red}{x} - \int \textcolor{red}{x} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{\frac{1}{x} \, dx}$$
$$= x \ln x - \int 1 \, dx = x \ln x - x + C$$
$$= x(\ln x - 1) + C$$

Key insight: Sometimes integration by parts works even when there's "only one function" - we introduce a factor of 1 and apply the technique. This is especially common with logarithmic and inverse trigonometric functions.

Mastering Integration by Parts:

  • LIATE is your guide: Choose u following Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential
  • Practice makes perfect: The more you recognize the patterns, the faster you'll choose u and dv
  • Multiple applications: Don't be surprised if you need to apply the technique several times
  • Circular integrals: When you get back to the original integral, solve algebraically
  • Check your work: Differentiate your answer to verify it matches the original integrand
