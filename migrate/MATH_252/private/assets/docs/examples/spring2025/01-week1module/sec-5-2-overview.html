This section introduces the definite integral, a fundamental concept in calculus that allows us to calculate the total signed area between a function and the x-axis over a specific interval.

In this section, we'll explore how the definite integral relates to finding areas under curves and its applications in physics, particularly in understanding displacement from velocity. We'll start with simple examples and build up to more complex scenarios.

Definition: The Definite Integral

The definite integral of a function \( f(x) \) from \( a \) to \( b \) is defined as the total signed area between the curve and the x-axis over the interval \([a,b]\), denoted as:

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]

where:

  • The total signed area is the area above the x-axis minus the area below the x-axis
  • \( a \) and \( b \) are called the bounds of integration

Key Properties of Definite Integrals

  1. Zero Width Property:

    \[ \int_a^a f(x)\,dx = 0 \]

  2. Additive Property:

    \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx + \int_b^c f(x)\,dx = \int_a^c f(x)\,dx \]

  3. Reversal of Bounds:

    \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = -\int_b^a f(x)\,dx \]

  4. Constant Multiple:

    \[ \int_a^b c f(x)\,dx = c \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]

Example: Finding Displacement from Velocity

Consider an object moving in a straight line with velocity \( v(t) = 5 \) ft/s for 10 seconds, then reversing direction at \( -2 \) ft/s for 4 seconds. The total displacement is:

\[ \text{Displacement} = \int_0^{10} 5\,dt + \int_{10}^{14} (-2)\,dt = 50 - 8 = 42\text{ feet} \]

This example shows how the definite integral of velocity gives displacement, with areas above the t-axis contributing positively and areas below contributing negatively.
