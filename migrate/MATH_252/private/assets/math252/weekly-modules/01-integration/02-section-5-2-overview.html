Section 5.2: The Definite Integral

📚 Required Reading: APEX Calculus - Section 5.2

This section introduces the definite integral, a powerful concept that quantifies accumulated change and allows us to calculate areas under curves.

Introduction to Definite Integrals

In Section 5.1, we explored antiderivatives and the indefinite integral. Now we'll build on those concepts to develop the definite integral, which has a geometric interpretation as the area under a curve.

The definite integral gives us a way to calculate accumulated change over an interval. This has powerful applications in physics, engineering, economics, and many other fields. For example, in physics, if we know the velocity of an object over time, we can use a definite integral to find the total displacement.

Definition: The Definite Integral

Let \(f\) be a continuous function on the interval \([a,b]\). The definite integral of \(f\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is defined as:

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]

The definite integral represents the net signed area between the curve \(y = f(x)\) and the x-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\). Areas above the x-axis count as positive, while areas below the x-axis count as negative.

See full definition in the textbook

Unlike the indefinite integral, the definite integral results in a number, not a function. The definite integral measures accumulated change over a specific interval.

Properties of Definite Integrals

Theorem: Properties of Definite Integrals

Let \(f\) and \(g\) be continuous functions on \([a,b]\) and let \(c\) be a constant. The following properties hold:

  1. Zero Width Property: \[\int_a^a f(x)\,dx = 0\]

    Geometric Interpretation: The integral over an interval of zero width is zero because there's no horizontal space to calculate any area. Picture a vertical line segment at \(x = a\) – it has height but no width, so its area is zero.

  2. Reverse Limits: \[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = -\int_b^a f(x)\,dx\]

    Geometric Interpretation: Reversing the limits of integration changes the sign of the integral. Think of this as traveling through the area in the opposite direction – if moving left-to-right counts areas as positive, then moving right-to-left counts the same areas as negative.

  3. Constant Multiple: \[\int_a^b c \cdot f(x)\,dx = c \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\]

    Geometric Interpretation: Constants can be factored out of the integral. Visually, this is like scaling the height of the function by a factor of \(c\). If we double the height of a function (\(c=2\)), we double the area under the curve.

  4. Sum/Difference: \[\int_a^b [f(x) \pm g(x)]\,dx = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \pm \int_a^b g(x)\,dx\]

    Geometric Interpretation: The integral of a sum is the sum of the integrals. Geometrically, this means adding or subtracting the corresponding areas. If you stack two functions on top of each other, the total area equals the sum of their individual areas.

  5. Additivity of Intervals: \[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx + \int_b^c f(x)\,dx = \int_a^c f(x)\,dx\]

    Geometric Interpretation: The integral over adjacent intervals can be split or combined. This is like cutting a large area into smaller pieces at point \(b\). The area from \(a\) to \(c\) equals the sum of the area from \(a\) to \(b\) plus the area from \(b\) to \(c\).

Physical Applications: Displacement from Velocity

One of the most important applications of the definite integral is in physics, where it helps us understand motion. If we know the velocity function \(v(t)\) of an object moving along a straight line, the definite integral of velocity gives us the displacement (change in position) over a time interval.

Example: Finding Displacement from Velocity

A particle moves along a straight line with velocity function \(v(t) = 3t^2 - 5t + 2\) (in meters per second), where \(t\) is measured in seconds. Find the displacement of the particle during the time interval \([1, 4]\).

Solution:

The displacement is given by the definite integral of velocity:

\begin{align} \text{Displacement} &= \int_1^4 v(t)\,dt \\ &= \int_1^4 (3t^2 - 5t + 2)\,dt \\ &= \left[ t^3 - \frac{5t^2}{2} + 2t \right]_1^4 \\ &= \left[ 4^3 - \frac{5(4^2)}{2} + 2(4) \right] - \left[ 1^3 - \frac{5(1^2)}{2} + 2(1) \right] \\ &= \left[ 64 - 40 + 8 \right] - \left[ 1 - 2.5 + 2 \right] \\ &= 32 - 0.5 \\ &= 31.5 \text{ meters} \end{align}

So the particle moves 31.5 meters during the time interval from \(t = 1\) to \(t = 4\).

Example: Understanding Motion with Changing Direction

A car moves along a straight road with velocity \(v(t) = t^2 - 4t + 3\) (in meters per second) for \(0 \leq t \leq 5\).

  1. When is the car moving forward (positive velocity), and when is it moving backward (negative velocity)?
  2. Find the total displacement of the car during the 5-second interval.
  3. Find the total distance traveled by the car during the 5-second interval.

Solution:

1. Finding when the car changes direction:

The car moves forward when \(v(t) > 0\) and backward when \(v(t) < 0\). To find when the velocity is zero: \[v(t) = t^2 - 4t + 3 = (t-1)(t-3) = 0\] So \(v(t) = 0\) when \(t = 1\) or \(t = 3\).

Testing values in different intervals, we can determine:

  • For \(0 \leq t < 1\): \(v(t) > 0\), car moves forward
  • For \(1 < t < 3\): \(v(t) < 0\), car moves backward
  • For \(3 < t \leq 5\): \(v(t) > 0\), car moves forward again

2. Total displacement (net change in position):

\begin{align} \text{Displacement} &= \int_0^5 v(t)\,dt \\ &= \int_0^5 (t^2 - 4t + 3)\,dt \\ &= \left[ \frac{t^3}{3} - 2t^2 + 3t \right]_0^5 \\ &= \left[ \frac{5^3}{3} - 2(5^2) + 3(5) \right] - \left[ 0 \right] \\ &= \frac{125}{3} - 50 + 15 \\ &= \frac{125}{3} - 35 \\ &= \frac{125 - 105}{3} \\ &= \frac{20}{3} \approx 6.67 \text{ meters} \end{align}

3. Total distance traveled:

To find the total distance (accounting for both forward and backward motion), we need to integrate the absolute value of velocity: \[\text{Distance} = \int_0^5 |v(t)|\,dt\] We'll split this into three intervals based on the sign of \(v(t)\): \[\begin{align} \text{Distance} &= \int_0^1 v(t)\,dt + \int_1^3 -v(t)\,dt + \int_3^5 v(t)\,dt \\ &= \int_0^1 (t^2 - 4t + 3)\,dt + \int_1^3 -(t^2 - 4t + 3)\,dt + \int_3^5 (t^2 - 4t + 3)\,dt \end{align}\] Evaluating these integrals and adding them gives us the total distance of approximately 10.67 meters.

Geometric Interpretation of the Definite Integral

The definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) represents the net signed area between the curve \(y = f(x)\) and the x-axis over the interval \([a,b]\).

Signed area means:

  • Regions where \(f(x) > 0\) (above the x-axis) contribute positive area
  • Regions where \(f(x) < 0\) (below the x-axis) contribute negative area
  • The definite integral gives the algebraic sum of these areas

Key Takeaways

  • The definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) gives us the net signed area between the function and the x-axis over the interval \([a,b]\)
  • Definite integrals represent accumulated change over an interval
  • In physics, the definite integral of velocity gives displacement
  • Properties of definite integrals let us manipulate and evaluate integrals more easily
  • The total distance traveled is found by integrating the absolute value of velocity
