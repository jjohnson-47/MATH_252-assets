The Big Picture: Strategy for Trigonometric Integrals

Trigonometric integrals require a strategic approach based on the specific combination of functions and powers involved. Unlike substitution or integration by parts, there's no single universal method—instead, we need a toolbox of techniques.

The key insight: We use trigonometric identities to transform complex trigonometric expressions into forms we can integrate using basic rules or substitution techniques.

Essential Trigonometric Identities

Power Reduction Formulas

These identities are crucial for handling even powers of sine and cosine:

$$\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2}$$
$$\cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}$$

Why these work: They reduce the power from 2 to 1, making integration possible using basic antiderivative rules.

Pythagorean Identities

Essential for working with odd powers and substitution:

$$\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$$
$$1 + \tan^2 x = \sec^2 x$$
$$1 + \cot^2 x = \csc^2 x$$

Product-to-Sum Formulas

Used when integrating products of different trigonometric functions:

$$\sin A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A+B) + \sin(A-B)]$$
$$\cos A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A+B) + \cos(A-B)]$$
$$\sin A \sin B = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)]$$

Integration Strategies

Powers of Sine and Cosine: Decision Tree

Case Strategy Key Technique
Odd power of sine
$\int \sin^{2n+1} x \cos^m x \, dx$		 Factor out $\textcolor{blue}{\sin x}$, convert remaining $\sin^{2n} x$ to $\textcolor{red}{\cos^{2n} x}$ Substitution: $\textcolor{red}{u = \cos x}$, $\textcolor{blue}{du = -\sin x \, dx}$
Odd power of cosine
$\int \sin^m x \cos^{2n+1} x \, dx$		 Factor out $\textcolor{blue}{\cos x}$, convert remaining $\cos^{2n} x$ to $\textcolor{red}{\sin^{2n} x}$ Substitution: $\textcolor{red}{u = \sin x}$, $\textcolor{blue}{du = \cos x \, dx}$
Both powers even
$\int \sin^{2m} x \cos^{2n} x \, dx$		 Use power reduction formulas repeatedly $\sin^2 x = \frac{1-\cos(2x)}{2}$, $\cos^2 x = \frac{1+\cos(2x)}{2}$
Both powers odd
$\int \sin^{2m+1} x \cos^{2n+1} x \, dx$		 Factor out either $\sin x$ or $\cos x$ (choose the lower power) Use substitution as above

Fundamental Examples

Example 1: Odd Power of Sine

Problem: Evaluate $\int \sin^3 x \cos^2 x \, dx$.

Step 1: Identify the strategy.

We have an odd power of sine (3) and an even power of cosine (2).

Strategy: Factor out $\textcolor{blue}{\sin x}$ and use substitution.

Step 2: Factor and convert using Pythagorean identity.

$$\int \sin^3 x \cos^2 x \, dx = \int \sin^2 x \cos^2 x \cdot \textcolor{blue}{\sin x} \, dx$$
$$= \int \textcolor{red}{(1 - \cos^2 x)} \cos^2 x \cdot \textcolor{blue}{\sin x} \, dx$$
$$= \int \textcolor{red}{(\cos^2 x - \cos^4 x)} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{\sin x} \, dx$$

Step 3: Apply substitution.

Let $\textcolor{red}{u = \cos x}$, then $\textcolor{blue}{du = -\sin x \, dx}$, so $\textcolor{blue}{\sin x \, dx = -du}$

$$= \int \textcolor{red}{(u^2 - u^4)} \cdot \textcolor{blue}{(-du)} = -\int (u^2 - u^4) \, du$$

Step 4: Integrate and substitute back.

$$= -\left(\frac{u^3}{3} - \frac{u^5}{5}\right) + C = -\frac{u^3}{3} + \frac{u^5}{5} + C$$
$$= -\frac{\cos^3 x}{3} + \frac{\cos^5 x}{5} + C$$

Example 2: Both Powers Even

Problem: Evaluate $\int \sin^2 x \cos^2 x \, dx$.

Step 1: Identify the strategy.

Both powers are even, so we need power reduction formulas.

Step 2: Apply power reduction formulas.

$$\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2}, \quad \cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}$$
$$\int \sin^2 x \cos^2 x \, dx = \int \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2} \cdot \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2} \, dx$$

Step 3: Expand the product.

$$= \frac{1}{4} \int (1 - \cos(2x))(1 + \cos(2x)) \, dx$$
$$= \frac{1}{4} \int (1 - \cos^2(2x)) \, dx = \frac{1}{4} \int \sin^2(2x) \, dx$$

Step 4: Apply power reduction again.

$$\sin^2(2x) = \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{2}$$
$$= \frac{1}{4} \int \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{2} \, dx = \frac{1}{8} \int (1 - \cos(4x)) \, dx$$
$$= \frac{1}{8}\left(x - \frac{\sin(4x)}{4}\right) + C = \frac{x}{8} - \frac{\sin(4x)}{32} + C$$

Example 3: Product of Different Functions

Problem: Evaluate $\int \sin(3x) \cos(5x) \, dx$.

Step 1: Apply product-to-sum formula.

$$\sin A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A+B) + \sin(A-B)]$$

With $A = 3x$ and $B = 5x$:

$$\sin(3x) \cos(5x) = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(8x) + \sin(-2x)] = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(8x) - \sin(2x)]$$

Step 2: Integrate term by term.

$$\int \sin(3x) \cos(5x) \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \int [\sin(8x) - \sin(2x)] \, dx$$
$$= \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{\cos(8x)}{8} + \frac{\cos(2x)}{2}\right] + C$$
$$= -\frac{\cos(8x)}{16} + \frac{\cos(2x)}{4} + C$$

Mastering Trigonometric Integrals:

  • Strategy first: Identify the pattern before choosing your technique
  • Odd powers = substitution: Factor out one function and substitute
  • Even powers = reduction: Use power reduction formulas to lower the degree
  • Different functions = product-to-sum: Convert products to sums for easier integration
  • Practice pattern recognition: These become intuitive with experience
  • Check your work: Trigonometric derivatives can verify your antiderivatives
